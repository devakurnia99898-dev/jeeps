## The Diagnostic / The Situation
The 3% rule is a critical guideline for Jeep owners who are considering swapping to larger or smaller tires. This rule states that the overall diameter of the new tires should not exceed 3% of the original equipment (OE) tire diameter. Failure to adhere to this rule can result in costly repairs, voided warranties, and compromised vehicle performance. As a Master Jeep Mechanic and Off-road Engineer, I have seen numerous cases where owners have ignored this rule, only to face severe consequences.

Symptoms or signs that you may have exceeded the 3% rule include abnormal wear patterns on the tires, vibrations or shaking while driving, and decreased fuel efficiency. In severe cases, you may experience damage to the vehicle's drivetrain, suspension, or electrical systems.

## Technical Deep Dive
The 3% rule is based on the relationship between the tire's diameter and the vehicle's gearing. When you swap to larger or smaller tires, you are effectively changing the gear ratio of the vehicle. This can put additional stress on the drivetrain, suspension, and electrical systems, leading to premature wear and failure. The plastic impeller in the water pump, for example, can become damaged due to the increased load on the engine.

Common failure points associated with ignoring the 3% rule include the transfer case, axle shafts, and wheel bearings. The transfer case can become damaged due to the increased stress on the gears, while the axle shafts can become prone to breakage. The wheel bearings can also become worn out prematurely, leading to costly repairs.

## Step-by-Step Guide
### Step 1: Measure the Original Equipment Tire Diameter
To apply the 3% rule, you need to measure the diameter of the original equipment tires. This can be done using a tape measure or a caliper. Record the measurement and use it as a reference point for the new tires.

#### Step 1.1: Calculate the 3% Rule
To calculate the 3% rule, multiply the original equipment tire diameter by 0.03. This will give you the maximum allowed diameter increase or decrease.

#### Step 1.2: Choose the New Tires
Using the calculated 3% rule, choose new tires that are within the allowed diameter range. Consider factors such as tread pattern, load rating, and speed rating when making your selection.

### Step 2: Inspect the Vehicle's Suspension and Drivetrain
Before installing the new tires, inspect the vehicle's suspension and drivetrain for any signs of wear or damage. Check the transfer case, axle shafts, and wheel bearings for any unusual play or noise.

#### Step 2.1: Check the Transfer Case
The transfer case is a critical component that can be affected by the 3% rule. Check the transfer case for any signs of wear or damage, such as leaks or unusual noises.

#### Step 2.2: Check the Axle Shafts
The axle shafts can become prone to breakage if the 3% rule is exceeded. Check the axle shafts for any signs of wear or damage, such as cracks or excessive play.

### Step 3: Install the New Tires
Once you have selected the new tires and inspected the vehicle's suspension and drivetrain, you can proceed with the installation. Make sure to follow proper torque specs and balancing procedures to avoid any issues.

#### Step 3.1: Mount the New Tires
Mount the new tires on the wheels, making sure to follow the manufacturer's instructions. Use a torque wrench to tighten the lug nuts to the recommended spec.

#### Step 3.2: Balance the Wheels
Balance the wheels to ensure that they are properly aligned and weighted. This will help to prevent any vibrations or shaking while driving.
