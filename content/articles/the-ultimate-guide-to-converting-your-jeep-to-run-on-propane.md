Symptoms or signs that your Jeep may be a good candidate for a propane conversion include high fuel costs, frequent oil changes, and a desire to reduce your carbon footprint. If you are experiencing any of these issues, a propane conversion may be a good option for you.

## Technical Deep Dive
Propane is a byproduct of natural gas processing and crude oil refining. It is a clean-burning fuel that produces fewer emissions than regular gas and diesel. Propane-powered vehicles use a specialized engine and fuel system that is designed to run on propane. The engine is typically modified to include a propane-specific fuel injector and ignition system.

The propane fuel system consists of a tank, fuel lines, and a regulator. The tank is typically mounted in the bed of the Jeep, and the fuel lines run from the tank to the engine. The regulator is used to regulate the flow of propane to the engine.

Common failure points in a propane fuel system include the regulator, fuel lines, and tank. The regulator can fail due to corrosion or wear and tear, while the fuel lines can become damaged or clogged. The tank can also become corroded or damaged, which can lead to leaks and other safety issues.

Before starting the conversion, it is essential to plan and research the process. This includes determining the type of propane system to use, the size of the tank, and the location of the tank and fuel lines.
