## Technical Deep Dive
The Jeep's engine, suspension, and electrical systems are intricately connected. The engine, for example, relies on the electrical system to function correctly. A faulty alternator or battery can cause the engine to stall or fail to start. Similarly, the suspension system plays a critical role in maintaining tire contact with the road, which affects traction, handling, and overall safety.

Common failure points include the plastic impeller in the water pump, which can crack or break, causing coolant leaks and engine overheating. The suspension system's ball joints and control arms are also prone to wear and tear, leading to uneven tire wear and decreased handling.

## Step-by-Step Guide
### Step 1: Oil Change
The oil change is a straightforward process that can be done at home with basic tools. Start by locating the oil drain plug, usually found at the bottom of the engine. Use a 10mm socket to loosen the plug, then allow the oil to drain into a pan.

#### Sub-step 1.1: Locate the Oil Filter
The oil filter is usually located on top of the engine or on the side. Use a filter wrench to loosen and remove the filter.

#### Sub-step 1.2: Replace the Oil Filter
Install a new oil filter, making sure not to overtighten.

#### Sub-step 1.3: Refill the Oil
Use a funnel to pour in the new oil, making sure to check the owner's manual for the correct type and amount.

### Step 2: Tire Rotation
Tire rotation is an essential maintenance task that helps extend the life of your tires. Start by loosening the lug nuts on the first wheel using a torque wrench.

#### Sub-step 2.1: Raise the Vehicle
Use a jack to raise the vehicle, making sure to support it with jack stands for safety.

#### Sub-step 2.2: Remove the Wheel
Remove the lug nuts and flat washers, then pull the wheel straight off.

#### Sub-step 2.3: Rotate the Tires
Rotate the tires according to the recommended pattern, usually found in the owner's manual.

### Step 3: Brake Pad Replacement
Brake pad replacement is a more complex task that requires specialized tools. Start by locating the brake caliper, usually found on the wheel hub.

#### Sub-step 3.1: Remove the Caliper
Use a wrench to loosen the caliper bolts, then pull the caliper off the rotor.

#### Sub-step 3.2: Remove the Old Brake Pads
Pull out the old brake pads, taking note of the wear sensor wire.

#### Sub-step 3.3: Install the New Brake Pads
Install the new brake pads, making sure to secure them with the retaining clips.

## Pro Tips & Common Mistakes
Don't overtighten the oil drain plug, as this can cause damage to the threads. Additionally, avoid using the wrong type of oil, as this can lead to engine damage.

Tricks of the trade include using a torque wrench to ensure accurate torque specs and keeping a maintenance log to track your progress.

## Cost Analysis: DIY vs Mechanic
The cost of DIY maintenance can be significantly lower than taking your vehicle to a mechanic. For example, an oil change can cost as little as $50, while a mechanic may charge upwards of $100.

However, more complex tasks like brake pad replacement may require specialized tools and expertise, making it more cost-effective to take your vehicle to a mechanic.

## Frequently Asked Questions
1. **What is the recommended oil change interval for my Jeep?**
The recommended oil change interval varies depending on the model and year of your Jeep. Check your owner's manual for specific guidance.
2. **How often should I rotate my tires?**
Tire rotation should be done every 5,000 to 8,000 miles, depending on the type of vehicle and driving conditions.
3. **What are the symptoms of a faulty alternator?**
A faulty alternator can cause a range of symptoms, including dim or flickering headlights, a dead battery, and strange electrical noises.
4. **Can I replace my brake pads myself?**
While it's possible to replace brake pads yourself, it's recommended to seek the help of a professional mechanic, especially if you're not experienced with brake work.
5. **How often should I check my suspension system?**
The suspension system should be checked every 12,000 to 15,000 miles, or as recommended by the manufacturer.
