## Technical Deep Dive
Vehicle detection systems at traffic lights typically use a combination of sensors, including inductive loops, video cameras, radar sensors, and infrared sensors. Inductive loops are embedded in the road surface and detect the presence of vehicles by measuring the change in inductance caused by the vehicle's presence. Video cameras use computer vision algorithms to detect vehicles and track their movement. Radar sensors use microwave radiation to detect the speed and distance of vehicles, while infrared sensors detect the heat emitted by vehicles.

Common failure points in vehicle detection systems include faulty sensors, damaged or corroded sensor cables, and incorrect sensor calibration. The inductive loops can be damaged by road construction or heavy vehicle traffic, while video cameras can be affected by weather conditions or vandalism. Radar sensors can be interfered with by other radar sources, such as police radar guns, while infrared sensors can be affected by changes in ambient temperature.

## Step-by-Step Guide
### Step 1: Identify the Type of Sensor
Identify the type of sensor used in the vehicle detection system. This can be done by consulting the traffic light's documentation or by visually inspecting the sensor.

#### Step 1.1: Inspect the Sensor
Inspect the sensor for any visible damage or signs of wear. Check for corrosion, cracks, or other damage that could affect the sensor's performance.

#### Step 1.2: Consult Documentation
Consult the traffic light's documentation to determine the type of sensor used and its specifications.

### Step 2: Test the Sensor
Test the sensor to determine if it is functioning correctly. This can be done using specialized test equipment, such as an inductive loop detector or a video camera tester.

#### Step 2.1: Connect Test Equipment
Connect the test equipment to the sensor and follow the manufacturer's instructions for testing.

#### Step 2.2: Analyze Results
Analyze the results of the test to determine if the sensor is functioning correctly. If the sensor is not functioning correctly, proceed to the next step.

### Step 3: Repair or Replace the Sensor
Repair or replace the sensor as needed. This can involve replacing damaged or corroded sensor cables, recalibrating the sensor, or replacing the sensor entirely.

#### Step 3.1: Replace Sensor Cables
Replace damaged or corroded sensor cables with new ones. Ensure that the new cables are properly connected and secured.

#### Step 3.2: Recalibrate Sensor
Recalibrate the sensor according to the manufacturer's instructions. This can involve adjusting the sensor's sensitivity or calibration settings.

#### Step 3.3: Replace Sensor
Replace the sensor entirely if it is damaged beyond repair. Ensure that the new sensor is properly installed and calibrated.

## Pro Tips & Common Mistakes
* Use proper test equipment to avoid damaging the sensor or the traffic light.
* Avoid over-tightening sensor cables, as this can damage the sensor or the traffic light.
* Ensure that the sensor is properly calibrated to avoid false detections or missed detections.
* Don't forget to test the sensor after repair or replacement to ensure that it is functioning correctly.
