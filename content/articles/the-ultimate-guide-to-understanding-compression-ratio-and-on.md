## The Diagnostic / The Situation
The compression ratio of an engine is a critical factor that affects its efficiency, power output, and fuel consumption. In simple terms, the compression ratio is the ratio of the volume of the cylinder when the piston is at the bottom of its stroke (called the bottom dead center) to the volume of the cylinder when the piston is at the top of its stroke (called the top dead center). A higher compression ratio means that the air-fuel mixture in the cylinder is compressed to a smaller volume, resulting in a more efficient combustion process.

Symptoms of a low compression ratio include decreased engine power, reduced fuel efficiency, and increased emissions. On the other hand, a high compression ratio can lead to increased engine power, improved fuel efficiency, and reduced emissions. However, it also increases the risk of engine knock or pinging, which can damage the engine over time.

## Technical Deep Dive
The compression ratio of an engine is determined by the design of the cylinder head, piston, and crankshaft. The cylinder head design, including the shape and size of the combustion chamber, plays a significant role in determining the compression ratio. The piston design, including the shape and size of the piston crown, also affects the compression ratio. The crankshaft design, including the stroke length and connecting rod length, also influences the compression ratio.

Common failure points that can affect the compression ratio include worn or damaged piston rings, cylinder head gasket failure, and cracked or damaged cylinder walls. These issues can lead to a decrease in compression ratio, resulting in reduced engine efficiency and power output.

#### Step 2.1: Thread the Compression Gauge
