## The Diagnostic / The Situation
The technical background of super clipping lies in the car's aerodynamics, suspension, and tire dynamics. When a driver takes a corner, the car's aerodynamic downforce plays a significant role in determining its grip and stability. By clipping the kerb, the driver can momentarily increase the car's downforce, allowing it to corner faster. However, this technique also puts immense stress on the car's suspension and tires, which can lead to premature wear and potential failures. Symptoms of super clipping include unusual tire wear patterns, suspension damage, and decreased car stability.

## Technical Deep Dive
To truly understand super clipping, we need to dive deeper into the technical aspects of a Formula 1 car. The suspension system, comprising springs, dampers, and anti-roll bars, plays a critical role in maintaining the car's stability and grip. The aerodynamic package, including the front and rear wings, also significantly impacts the car's downforce and drag. When a driver super clips a corner, the car's suspension is subjected to extreme loads, which can cause the tires to lose grip and the car to become unstable. Common failure points in super clipping include the suspension's pushrods, track rods, and wheel bearings.
