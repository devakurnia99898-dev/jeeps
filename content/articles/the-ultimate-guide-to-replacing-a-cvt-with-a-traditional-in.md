## Technical Deep Dive
A CVT uses a belt and pulley system to provide an infinite number of gear ratios. This allows the engine to stay within its optimal power range, resulting in improved fuel efficiency. However, the CVT's complex design and reliance on electronic controls can make it prone to failures.

The eight-speed automatic transmission, on the other hand, uses a more traditional gear set with a torque converter to provide smooth and efficient shifting. This type of transmission is generally more reliable and easier to repair than a CVT.

Common failure points in a CVT include:
* The belt and pulley system, which can wear out over time
* The electronic controls, which can malfunction due to software or hardware issues
* The transmission's cooling system, which can fail due to clogged lines or faulty pumps

## Step-by-Step Guide
### Step 1: Prepare the Vehicle
#### Remove the transmission pan and drain the fluid
Use a 10mm socket to remove the transmission pan bolts and carefully lower the pan to avoid spilling fluid. Use a funnel to drain the fluid into a pan.

#### Remove the transmission cooler lines
Use a wrench to loosen the cooler line fittings and carefully pull the lines off the transmission.

#### Remove the transmission mounts
Use a socket to remove the transmission mount bolts and carefully pull the mounts off the transmission.

### Step 2: Remove the CVT
#### Remove the CVT's electrical connectors
Use a wrench to loosen the electrical connector fittings and carefully pull the connectors off the CVT.

#### Remove the CVT's mounting bolts
Use a socket to remove the CVT's mounting bolts and carefully pull the CVT out of the vehicle.
