One of the most important considerations is the engine's power-to-weight ratio. A smaller engine can be made to produce a significant amount of power relative to its size, but it requires careful planning and execution. This can be achieved through the use of high-performance engine components, such as forged pistons, connecting rods, and crankshafts, as well as careful engine tuning and calibration. However, there are also several potential pitfalls to watch out for, including overheating, oil starvation, and engine damage due to excessive stress.

## Technical Deep Dive
A high-performance, small-displacement engine is a complex system that requires careful consideration of several key components, including the engine block, cylinder head, crankshaft, camshaft, and valvetrain. The engine block provides the foundation for the engine, and must be strong and durable enough to withstand the stresses of high-performance operation. The cylinder head, on the other hand, plays a critical role in determining the engine's power output, and must be carefully designed and optimized to provide the best possible combination of flow and compression.

One of the most critical components of a high-performance, small-displacement engine is the crankshaft. The crankshaft converts the up-and-down motion of the pistons into rotary motion, and must be strong and durable enough to withstand the stresses of high-performance operation. A common failure point in high-performance engines is the crankshaft, particularly the main bearings and the connecting rod bearings. To minimize the risk of crankshaft failure, it's essential to use high-quality, forged crankshaft components, and to carefully monitor engine oil pressure and temperature.

## Step-by-Step Guide
### Step 1: Engine Disassembly
The first step in building a high-performance, small-displacement engine is to disassemble the engine and inspect the components. This involves removing the cylinder head, pistons, connecting rods, and crankshaft, and carefully inspecting each component for signs of wear or damage.
#### Step 1.1: Remove the Cylinder Head
To remove the cylinder head, start by loosening the head bolts in a star pattern. Once the head bolts are loose, use a puller to remove the head from the engine block.
#### Step 1.2: Remove the Pistons and Connecting Rods
To remove the pistons and connecting rods, start by removing the piston pins and connecting rod bolts. Once the piston pins and connecting rod bolts are removed, use a puller to remove the pistons and connecting rods from the engine block.
#### Step 1.3: Remove the Crankshaft
To remove the crankshaft, start by loosening the main bearings and connecting rod bearings. Once the main bearings and connecting rod bearings are loose, use a puller to remove the crankshaft from the engine block.
### Step 2: Engine Inspection and Diagnosis
Once the engine is disassembled, the next step is to inspect and diagnose the components. This involves carefully examining each component for signs of wear or damage, and using specialized tools to diagnose any problems.
#### Step 2.1: Inspect the Cylinder Head
To inspect the cylinder head, start by examining the combustion chamber and valve seats for signs of wear or damage. Use a leak-down test to check for any leaks in the head gasket or valve seats.
#### Step 2.2: Inspect the Pistons and Connecting Rods
To inspect the pistons and connecting rods, start by examining the piston skirts and connecting rod bearings for signs of wear or damage. Use a micrometer to check the piston diameter and connecting rod length.
#### Step 2.3: Inspect the Crankshaft
To inspect the crankshaft, start by examining the main bearings and connecting rod bearings for signs of wear or damage. Use a dial indicator to check the crankshaft runout and bearing clearance.
### Step 3: Engine Rebuilding
Once the engine components have been inspected and diagnosed, the next step is to rebuild the engine. This involves installing new components, such as piston rings and bearings, and carefully assembling the engine.
#### Step 3.1: Install the Piston Rings
To install the piston rings, start by applying a thin layer of engine oil to the piston ring grooves. Then, use a piston ring compressor to install the rings onto the piston.
#### Step 3.2: Install the Connecting Rods
To install the connecting rods, start by applying a thin layer of engine oil to the connecting rod bearings. Then, use a connecting rod bolt stretch gauge to install the connecting rods onto the crankshaft.
#### Step 3.3: Install the Crankshaft
To install the crankshaft, start by applying a thin layer of engine oil to the main bearings and connecting rod bearings. Then, use a torque wrench to install the crankshaft onto the engine block.
### Step 4: Engine Tuning and Calibration
The final step in building a high-performance, small-displacement engine is to tune and calibrate the engine. This involves adjusting the engine's fuel injection and ignition systems to optimize power output and efficiency.
#### Step 4.1: Adjust the Fuel Injection System
To adjust the fuel injection system, start by checking the fuel pressure and flow rate. Then, use a fuel injection tuner to adjust the fuel injection mapping and optimize power output.
#### Step 4.2: Adjust the Ignition System
To adjust the ignition system, start by checking the ignition timing and spark voltage. Then, use an ignition tuner to adjust the ignition mapping and optimize power output.

## Pro Tips & Common Mistakes
When building a high-performance, small-displacement engine, there are several common mistakes to watch out for. One of the most common mistakes is to over-rev the engine, which can cause damage to the pistons, connecting rods, and crankshaft. Another common mistake is to under-tune the engine, which can result in poor power output and efficiency.
To avoid these mistakes, it's essential to carefully plan and execute the engine build, and to carefully monitor engine performance and adjust the tuning and calibration as needed. Here are some pro tips to keep in mind:
* Always use high-quality, forged engine components to minimize the risk of engine failure.
* Carefully monitor engine oil pressure and temperature to avoid overheating and oil starvation.
* Use a dyno to test and tune the engine, and to optimize power output and efficiency.
