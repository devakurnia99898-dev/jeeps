## Step-by-Step Guide
### Preparation
The first step in restoring a classic car is to thoroughly inspect the vehicle and identify areas that require attention. This includes checking the engine, transmission, suspension, and brakes, as well as the electrical and interior systems.

#### Inspecting the Engine
Check the engine for any signs of damage or wear, including oil leaks, cracked cylinders, and worn piston rings. Use a compression test to determine the engine's overall health.

#### Inspecting the Transmission
Check the transmission for any signs of wear or damage, including cracked gears, worn bearings, and leaks. Use a leak-down test to determine the transmission's overall health.

### Disassembly
Once the inspection is complete, the next step is to disassemble the car, removing all components, including the engine, transmission, suspension, and brakes. This will allow for a thorough cleaning and inspection of each component.

#### Removing the Engine
Use a hoist to lift the engine out of the car, taking care to support the engine and transmission. Remove all accessories, including the alternator, starter, and water pump.

#### Removing the Transmission
Use a transmission jack to lift the transmission out of the car, taking care to support the transmission and drivetrain. Remove all accessories, including the clutch and pressure plate.

### Cleaning and Inspection
With all components removed, the next step is to thoroughly clean and inspect each part, looking for any signs of wear or damage.

#### Cleaning the Engine
Use a parts washer to clean the engine, removing all dirt, grime, and debris. Inspect the engine for any signs of wear or damage, including cracked cylinders and worn piston rings.

#### Cleaning the Transmission
Use a parts washer to clean the transmission, removing all dirt, grime, and debris. Inspect the transmission for any signs of wear or damage, including cracked gears and worn bearings.

### Reassembly
With all components cleaned and inspected, the next step is to reassemble the car, installing all new components, including the engine, transmission, suspension, and brakes.

#### Installing the Engine
Use a hoist to lift the engine into the car, taking care to support the engine and transmission. Install all accessories, including the alternator, starter, and water pump. Torque all bolts to the specified torque, including the engine mounts (30 ft-lbs) and transmission mounts (40 ft-lbs).

#### Installing the Transmission
Use a transmission jack to lift the transmission into the car, taking care to support the transmission and drivetrain. Install all accessories, including the clutch and pressure plate. Torque all bolts to the specified torque, including the transmission mounts (40 ft-lbs) and drivetrain mounts (50 ft-lbs).

## Pro Tips & Common Mistakes
* Always use the correct torque specs when tightening bolts and fasteners.
* Never over-tighten bolts or fasteners, as this can cause damage to the component or surrounding material.
* Use a wiring diagram to ensure all electrical connections are correct and secure.
* Never skip the inspection and testing phase, as this can lead to costly repairs down the road.

## Cost Analysis: DIY vs Mechanic
Restoring a classic car can be a costly endeavor, with prices ranging from $50,000 to $200,000 or more, depending on the extent of the restoration and the cost of parts and labor. DIY enthusiasts can save money by performing the work themselves, but this requires a significant amount of time and expertise. Hiring a professional mechanic or restoration shop can ensure a high-quality restoration, but this can be expensive, with labor costs ranging from $50 to $100 per hour.
